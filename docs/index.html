⚠ Invasive species alert

An invasive squirrel is closing in on our farm provinces.

Finlayson’s squirrel isn’t native to the Philippines — and with no natural predators here, it’s spreading from Metro Manila toward the fruit farms of Cavite, Batangas and Nueva Ecija. One photo and a pin from you help DENR find them before they reach the harvest.

Why this matters

A “harmless” squirrel with no brakes

Finlayson’s squirrel (Callosciurus finlaysonii) was introduced through the illegal exotic-pet trade. It breeds 2–4 young several times a year, and the Philippines has no predator or disease to keep it in check. The DENR has classified it as a destructive Invasive Alien Species.

🥭

It eats the harvest

Aggressively frugivorous — it strips mango, lanzones, rambutan and coconut, the exact crops provincial farmers live on.

🐦

It raids native birds

Documented robbing nests, eating eggs and hatchlings, and competing with native birds like local parrots for food and cavities.

🌳

It kills trees

Strips bark to wear down its teeth, exposing mature trees to fatal disease and thinning native forest canopy.

⚡

It chews infrastructure

In the city it gnaws through electrical, internet and fibre-optic cables — causing outages and costly repairs.

🩺

It’s a health risk

Like many wild rodents it can carry pathogens and transmit diseases — including rabies — through bites or scratches.

🚫

Don’t catch & release

DENR warns the public never to relocate them — it only spreads the infestation. Report it instead, and let the experts handle containment humanely.

The clock is running

From gated villages to the farm belt

It started inside a few exclusive Metro Manila communities. It is no longer contained there — and every dry season it pushes further into the provinces that feed the country.

2010s · First sighting

Inside Metro Manila’s gated villages

First reported in secured green communities and golf courses — by 2013 there were encounters of a squirrel chasing a native tarsier.

Today · Established

Across Metro Manila

Now common in Makati, Muntinlupa, Quezon City and UP Diliman — well past the point of easy containment in the city.

Now · The frontier

Breaching into Cavite, Batangas, Rizal & Nueva Ecija

DENR-BMB has confirmed active sightings encroaching on Central Luzon’s farms. Catching 10 squirrels at this frontier is worth more than 100 in a Manila park.

This is winnable — it’s been done before

Your sighting is the data that stops it

Governments can’t check every backyard. Around the world, the deciding factor has been ordinary people reporting what they see — turning the community into an early-warning radar that maps where the invasion is heading next.

🇳🇱
The Netherlands

A close cousin, wiped out

Pallas’s squirrels (a relative of Finlayson’s) escaped a pet dealer and spread across 50 km². A live-trap, sterilise & rehome campaign — powered by door-to-door public reporting — cleared them.

✓ 249 caught · 100% eradication
🇮🇪
Ireland

Nature did the work

Decades of culling barely dented invasive grey squirrels. Protecting one native predator — the pine marten — collapsed grey populations while native reds bounced back.

✓ Greys vanished from recolonised woods
🇬🇧
United Kingdom

Hold the frontier

Rather than chase 2.5 million squirrels everywhere, volunteers map “buffer zones” at the invasion’s edge and push it back — using public sighting maps to aim every trap.

✓ Periphery-first, data-driven control

What that means for the Philippines

01

Target the periphery first

The most valuable reports are from the edges — Cavite, Batangas, Nueva Ecija — where small numbers can still be stopped before they ruin farms.

02

Do it humanely, build trust

Everywhere it worked, the public trusted the method. Containment is humane and centralised — to protect native birds and poor farmers’ livelihoods.

03

Press in the dry season

Trapping works best when wild fruit is scarce and baited traps are tempting. Steady year-round reports tell teams exactly where to focus.
